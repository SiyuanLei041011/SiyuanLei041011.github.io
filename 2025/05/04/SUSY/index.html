Weyl Spinors

Brief Review of the Dirac Equation and of Some Matrix Properties

Dirac Equation:

\[\gamma^\mu P_\mu\Psi=m\Psi~~\rm{or}~~ \not{\rm{P}}\Psi=m\Psi(\rm{use~Feynman's~slash~notation})\]

Here, \(P_\mu\) represents the differential operator \(i\partial_\mu\). Throughout this note we use natural unit, in which \(c=\hbar=1\).

Dirac Lagrangian:

\[\mathcal{L}=\overline{\Psi}(\gamma^\mu P_\mu-m)\Psi\]

where the bar over the spinor denotes the Dirac adjoint \(\overline{\Psi}=\Psi^\dagger\gamma^0\).

Dirac matrix:

There are several different representations for the Dirac matrix \(\gamma^0\) and \(\vec{\gamma}\). We choose representation

\[\gamma^0=\begin{pmatrix} 0 & \boldsymbol{1}\\ \boldsymbol{1}&0 \end{pmatrix}~~\vec{\gamma}=\begin{pmatrix} 0& -\vec{\sigma}\\ \vec{\sigma}&0\\ \end{pmatrix}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{1}\) stands for the \(2\times 2\) identity matrix. These can be used to define \(\gamma^\mu=(\gamma^0,\vec{\gamma})\). The corresponding quatity with a covariant index is defined as \(\gamma_\mu=\eta_{\mu\nu}\gamma^\nu=(\gamma^0,-\vec{\gamma})\), where \(\eta_{\mu\nu}=\rm{diag}(1,-1,-1,-1)\).

For \(\gamma_5\), we choose the chiral or Weyl representation

\[\gamma_5=\begin{pmatrix} \boldsymbol{1}&0\\ 0&-\boldsymbol{1} \end{pmatrix}\]

Pauli matrices:

\[\sigma^1=\begin{pmatrix} 0&1\\ 1&0\\ \end{pmatrix}~~~~ \sigma^2=\begin{pmatrix} 0&-i\\ i&0\\ \end{pmatrix}~~~~ \sigma^3=\begin{pmatrix} 1&0\\ 0&-1 \end{pmatrix}\]

They have the follwing properties:

  1. They are hermitian, i.e., \((\sigma^i)^\dagger=\sigma^i\)
  2. The product of any two Pauli matrix is given by \(\sigma^i\sigma^j=\boldsymbol{1}\delta^{ij}+i\epsilon^{ijk}\sigma^k\)
  3. The commutator of two Pauli matrices is \([\sigma^1,\sigma^j]=2i\epsilon^{ijk}\sigma^k\)
  4. The anticommutator of two Pauli matries is \(\{\sigma^i,\sigma^j\}=2\delta^{ij}\boldsymbol{1}\)
  5. \(\sigma^2\vec{\sigma}^T=-\vec{\sigma}\sigma^2,~~\sigma^2\vec{\sigma}^*=-\vec{\sigma}\sigma^2\)

Weyl versus Dirac Spinors

We now wirte the four-component Dirac spinor in terms of two-component spinors:

\[\Psi=\begin{pmatrix} \eta\\ \chi \end{pmatrix}\]

where the two-component spinors are called Weyl spinors. The reason it makes sense to decompose a Dirac spinor this way is that, as we will see, \(\eta\) and \(\chi\) transform independently under Lorentz transformations, i.e., they don’t mix. The technical way to express this is to say that a Dirac spinor forms a reducible representationpf the Lorentz group, whereas the Weyl spinors form irreducible representations.

Note that setting either \(\eta\) or \(\chi\) equal to zero in a Dirac spinor yields eigenstates of \(\gamma_5\):

\[\gamma_5\begin{pmatrix} \eta\\ 0 \end{pmatrix}=+\begin{pmatrix} \eta\\ 0 \end{pmatrix}~~~~\gamma_5\begin{pmatrix} 0\\ \chi \end{pmatrix}=-\begin{pmatrix} 0\\ \chi \end{pmatrix}\]

The eigenvalue of \(\gamma_5\) is called the chirality of the spinor, so we say that the upper two-component spinor \(\eta\) has a chirality of \(+1\) and \(\chi\) has a chirality of \(-1\). A Weyl spinor with positive chirality is sometimes referred to as a right-chiral spinor, and a Weyl spinor with negative chirality is referred to as a right-chiral spinor.

However, some references replace the adjectives right-chiral and left-chiral by right-handed and left-handed, but in general, knowing the chirality doesn’t tell us the handedness of a particle.

We will discuss how the handedness, or to be more technical, the helicity of a spinor is defined.

The key point is to make here is that the notions of chirality and the helicity of a spinor coincide only when a particle is massless. For a massive particle, both the left-chiral and the right-chiral states are, in general, linear combinations of left-handed and right-handed states.

It’s chirality that tells us how a spinor behaves under Lorentz transformations. For a massive spinor, it’s the chirality that specifies what representation of the Lorentz group it belongs to, not its helicity, so we will care more about chirality than about helicity.

It is convenint to introduce the operators \(P_R\) and \(P_L\), which, when applied to a fuul Dirac spinor, project out the right-chiral and left-chiral Weyl spinors:

\[P_R\Psi=P_R\begin{pmatrix} \eta\\ \chi\\ \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} \eta\\ 0 \end{pmatrix}~~~~P_L\Psi=P_L\begin{pmatrix} \eta\\ \chi\\ \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 0\\ \chi \end{pmatrix}\]

The explicit representations of these opeartors are obviously

\[P_R=\begin{pmatrix} \boldsymbol{1}&0\\ 0&0 \end{pmatrix}=\frac{\boldsymbol{1}+\gamma_5}{2}~~~~P_L=\begin{pmatrix} 0&0\\ 0&\boldsymbol{1} \end{pmatrix}=\frac{\boldsymbol{1}-\gamma_5}{2}\]

If we substitute \(\Psi=\begin{pmatrix} \eta\\ \chi \end{pmatrix}\) to the Dirac equation, we can obtain two coupled equations for the Weyl spinors:

\[\begin{aligned} (E\boldsymbol{1}-\vec{\sigma}\cdot\vec{p})\eta=m\chi\\ (E\boldsymbol{1}+\vec{\sigma}\cdot\vec{p})\chi=m\eta \end{aligned}\]

Now we introduce some useful notations:

\[\sigma^\mu=(\boldsymbol{1},\vec{\sigma}),~~\overline{\sigma}^\mu=(1,-\vec{\sigma})\]

we can check that they have the following properties:

  1. \(\sigma^2(\sigma^\mu)^T\sigma^2=\overline{\sigma}^\mu,~~\sigma^2(\overline{\sigma}^\mu)^T\sigma^2=\sigma^\mu\)
  2. \(\sigma^2\sigma^\mu\sigma^2=(\overline{\sigma}^\mu)^T,~~\sigma^2\overline{\sigma}^\mu\sigma^2=(\sigma^\mu)^T\)
  3. \(\sigma^\mu\overline{\sigma}^\nu+\sigma^\nu\overline{\sigma}^\mu=2\eta^{\mu\nu}\boldsymbol{1}\)
  4. \(\overline{\sigma}^\mu\sigma^\nu+\overline{\sigma}^\nu\sigma^\mu=2\eta^{\mu\nu}\boldsymbol{1}\)
  5. \(\gamma^\mu=\begin{pmatrix} 0&\overline{\sigma}^\mu\\ \sigma^\mu&0 \end{pmatrix}\)

So the two coupled equations may be written as

\[\begin{aligned} P_\mu\sigma^\mu\eta=m\chi\\ P_\mu\overline{\sigma}^\mu\chi=m\eta\\ \end{aligned}\]

and we can also write the Dirac lagrangianin terms of Weyl spinors:

\[\mathcal{L}=\eta^\dagger\sigma^\mu i\partial_\mu\eta+\chi^\dagger\overline{\sigma}^\mu i\partial_\mu\chi-m\eta^\dagger\chi-m\chi^\dagger\eta\]

Note that despide the fact that \(\sigma^\mu\) and \(\overline{\sigma}^\mu\) carry a Lorentz index, they are not themselves four-vectors (for the same reason that \(\gamma^\mu\) is not a four-vector). However, we can form quantities with well-defined Lorentz properties by sandwiching them between Weyl spinors.

Helicity

For massless particles, the two equations for Weyl spinors decouple to give:

\[\begin{aligned} (E\boldsymbol{1}-\vec{\sigma}\cdot\vec{p})\eta=0\\ (E\boldsymbol{1}+\vec{\sigma}\cdot\vec{p})\chi=0 \end{aligned}~~\Rightarrow~~\begin{aligned} \frac{\vec{\sigma}\cdot\vec{p}}{|\vec{p}|}\eta=\eta\\ \frac{\vec{\sigma}\cdot\vec{p}}{|\vec{p}|}\chi=-\chi \end{aligned}\]

where we have set \(E=|\vec{p}|\), which is valid for massless particles. We see that for a massless particle, the Dirac equation breaks up into two independent equations acting on two defferent two-component spinors. These two equations are known as the Weyl equations.

If we multiply those equations by \(\hbar/2\) and use the fact that \(\hbar\vec{\sigma}/2\) is the spin operator \(\vec{S}\), we get

\[\vec{S}\cdot\hat{p}\eta=\frac{\hbar}{2}\eta~~~~\vec{S}\cdot\hat{p}\chi=-\frac{\hbar}{2}\chi\]

The operator \(\vec{S}\cdot\hat{p}\) is called the helicity operator, and the corresponding eigenvalue is the helicity of the state. This eigenvalue corresponds to the component of the spin along the direction of the motion of the particle. We see that, in massless limit, thw Weyl spinors are eigenstates of the helicity operator.

Lorentz Transformations and Invariants

The infinitesimal transformations of the two Weyl spinors are:

\[\begin{aligned} \eta\rightarrow\eta'=\left(\boldsymbol{1}+\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}-\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma}\right)\eta\\ \chi\rightarrow\chi'=\left(\boldsymbol{1}+\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}+\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma}\right)\chi\\ \end{aligned}\]

where \(\vec{\epsilon}\) is the infinitesimal rotation vector (the direction of \(\vec{\epsilon}\) gives the axis of rotation, and its magnitude gives the amount of rotation), and \(\vec{\beta}\) is the infinitesimal boost parameter.

This transformations show that the left-chiral and right-chiral spinors transform independently under Lorentz transformations. They are therefore inequivalent irreducible representations of the Lorentz group and are the fundamental “building blocks” from which any other spinor representation can be constructed. Because Lorentz algebra is equivalent to the algebra \(\mathfrak{su}(2)\times\mathfrak{su}(2)\), all representations can be labeled by two numbers that are multiples of one-half. The two fundamental representations then are described by the quantum numbers \((\frac{1}{2},0)\) and \((0,\frac{1}{2})\), which describe the left-chiral and right-chiral spinors respectively. By the way, even though the algebra acting on the Weyl spinors is \(\mathfrak{su}(2)\times\mathfrak{su}(2)\), the group under which they transform is not \(SO(2)\times SO(2)\) but instead \(SL(2,\mathbb{C})\), the universal covering group of the Lorentz group \(SO(3,1)\).

Now consider the kinetic term of the Dirac lagrangian \(\overline{\Psi}\gamma^\mu P_\mu\Psi\). Since this is invariant and \(P_\mu\) is a four-vector, the quantity \(\overline{\Psi}\gamma^\mu\Psi\) must transform as a four-vector, so we find that

\[\overline{\Psi}\gamma^\mu\Psi=\eta^\dagger\sigma^\mu\eta+\chi^\dagger\overline{\sigma}^\mu\chi\]

transform as a four-vector under Lorentz transformations, and it turns out that each term on the right separately transform as a four-vector.

Building More Lorentz Invariants Out of Weyl Spinors

First we consider \(\chi^{\dagger T}\), we find that it transforms as

\[\begin{aligned} \chi^{\dagger T}\rightarrow (\chi^{\dagger T})'=(\chi')^{\dagger T} &=\left(\boldsymbol{1}+\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}+\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma}\right)^*\chi^{\dagger T}\\ &=\left(\boldsymbol{1}-\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}^*+\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma}^*\right)\chi^{\dagger T} \end{aligned}\]

This doesn’t transform like a right-chiral spinor. Now we use the equation \(\sigma^2\vec{\sigma}^*=-\vec{\sigma}\sigma^2\) and consider

\[\begin{aligned} i\sigma^2\chi^{\dagger T}\rightarrow (i\sigma^2\chi^{\dagger T})' &=(i\sigma^2)^*\left(\boldsymbol{1}+\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}+\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma}\right)^*\chi^{\dagger T}\\ &=(i\sigma^2)\left(\boldsymbol{1}-\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}^*+\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma}^*\right)\chi^{\dagger T}\\ &=\left(\boldsymbol{1}+\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}-\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma}\right)i\sigma^2\chi^{\dagger T} \end{aligned}\]

which is the transformation of right-chiral spinor! Since the expression \(\eta^\dagger\chi\) is invariant under Lorentz tranformations and \(i\sigma^2\chi^{\dagger T}\) transforms like \(\eta\), we can know that the following expression is also invariant:

\[\begin{aligned} (i\sigma^2\chi^{\dagger T})^T\chi&=\chi^T(i\sigma^2)^\dagger\chi\\ &=\chi^T(-i\sigma^2)\chi \end{aligned}\]

We also know that \(\chi^\dagger\eta\) is invariant, we obtain a second invariant containing only left-chiral spinors:

\[\chi^\dagger i\sigma\chi^{\dagger T}\]

Similarly, it’s easy to check that \(-i\sigma^2\eta^{\dagger T}\) transforms like a left-chiral spinor.

Define the matrix \(A\) as

\[A=\boldsymbol{1}+\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}-\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma}\]

so we have

\[A^\dagger=\boldsymbol{1}-\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}-\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma},~~A^{-1}=\boldsymbol{1}-\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}+\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma}\]

In turn, this implies

\[(A^{-1})^\dagger=(A^\dagger)^{-1}=\boldsymbol{1}+\frac{i}{2}\vec{\epsilon}\cdot\vec{\sigma}+\frac{1}{2}\vec{\beta}\cdot\vec{\sigma}\]

We can write:

\[\begin{aligned} \eta&\rightarrow A\eta\\ i\sigma^2\chi^{\dagger T}&\rightarrow A(i\sigma^2\chi^{\dagger T})\\ \chi&\rightarrow (A^\dagger)^{-1}\chi\\ -i\sigma^2\eta^{\dagger T}&\rightarrow (A^\dagger)^{-1}(-i\sigma\eta^{\dagger T}) \end{aligned}\]

This gives eight combinations, but it turns out that only six of those are independent, which be taken to be

\[\boxed{\begin{aligned} -\chi^T i\sigma^2\chi~~~~&\chi^\dagger i\sigma^2\chi^{\dagger T}\\ \chi^\dagger \eta~~~~&\eta^\dagger\chi\\ -\eta i\sigma^2\eta^{\dagger T}~~~~&\eta^Ti\sigma^2\eta \end{aligned}}\]

Invariants Containing Lorentz Indeces

We have seen, by looking at the Dirac equation written in terms of Weyl spinors, that \(\sigma^\mu i\partial_\mu\eta\) transforms like a left-chiral spinor and \(\overline{\sigma}^\mu i\partial_\mu\chi\) transforms like a right-chiral spinor.

To build Lorentz invariants containing derivatives, we simply have to use the expressions in the box above with the \(\chi\) appearing in those equations replaced by \(\sigma^\mu i\partial_\mu\eta\) or the \(\eta\) replaced by \(\overline{\sigma}^\mu i\partial_\mu\chi\).

As examples, we have

\[\begin{aligned} \chi^\dagger\eta&\rightarrow \chi^\dagger\overline{\sigma}^\mu i\partial_\mu \chi\\ \eta^\dagger\chi&\rightarrow(\overline{\sigma}^\mu i\partial_\mu\chi)^\dagger\chi=-i(\partial_\mu\chi^\dagger)\overline{\sigma}^\mu\chi \end{aligned}\]

As part of a lagrangian density, however, an integration by parts can be used to show that the terms above are equivalent to each other after discarding a surface term. We will always assume that the surface terms vanish so that we may write

\[\int \mathrm{d}^4xA\partial_\mu B=-\int\mathrm{d}^4x(\partial_\mu A)B\]

Introduce a New Notation

In practice, SUSY theorists work almost exclusively with left-chiral spinors, even when considering supersymmetric extensions of the standard model, so we really only need to focus on the two invariants built out of a left-chiral spinor:

\[\chi^\dagger i\sigma^2\chi^{\dagger T},~~\chi^T(-i\sigma^2)\chi\]

We define two new types of spinor dot products between left-chiral Weyl spinors:

\[\chi\cdot\chi=\chi^T(-i\sigma^2)\chi,~~\overline{\chi}\cdot\overline{\chi}=\chi^\dagger i\sigma^2\chi^{\dagger T}\]

The Physics of Weyl, Majorana, and Dirac Spinors

Supersymmetric theories are often written in terms of Majorana spinors, instead of Weyl spinors or Dirac spinors.

Charge Conjugation and Antiparticles for Dirac Spinors

Charge conjugation is the operation that turns a field describing a certain particle into a field describing the corresponding antiparticle.

Consider a Dirac spinor corresponding to a certain particle \(p\):

\[\Psi_p=\begin{pmatrix} \eta_p\\ \chi_p \end{pmatrix}\]

The antiparticle spinor is obtained by applying the charge-conjugation operator to the particle state, we may write

\[\Psi_p^c=\Psi_{\overline{p}}=\begin{aligned} \eta_{\overline{p}}\\ \chi_{\overline{p}} \end{aligned}\]

where \(c\) denotes charge conjugation.

The Supersymmetric Charges and Their Algebra

When a lagrangian is observed to possess some continuous symmetry, the natural thing to do is to find the charges generating the symmetry, also called the generators of the symmetry. Actually, as we will see, what really matters is the algebra obeyed by the charges, i.e., the commutation or anticommutation rules, not the charges themselves (as an aside, when the algebra involves anticommutators, it is often referred to as a graded Lie algebra).

Charges: General Discussion

We firet consider a scalar field operator \(\phi\). If a certain transformation is a symmetry of the theory, it must leave unchanged the expectation value \(\langle a|\phi|b\rangle\). Let us focus now on spacetime transformations, and we have \(\langle a'|\phi(x')|b'\rangle=\langle a|\phi(x)|b\rangle\). We now introduce a new field operator \(\phi'\) that we define by \(\langle a'|\phi(x')|b'\rangle=\langle a|\phi'(x')|b\rangle\), so we get \(\langle a|\phi'(x')|b\rangle=\langle a|\phi(x)|b\rangle\), thus \(\phi'(x')=\phi(x)\). This is exactly how a scalar field behaves under a Lorentz transformation. We write \(x'=x-\delta x\), so we have \(\phi'(x-\delta x)=\phi(x)\) or \(\phi'(x)=\phi(x+\delta x)\).

We now consider a field operator \(\mathcal{F}\) that transforms under some representations of the Lorentz group (a vector, a spinor, etc.). Then we have \(\langle a'|\mathcal{F}(x)|b'\rangle=S(\Lambda)\langle a|\mathcal{F}(x)|b\rangle\), where \(S(\Lambda)\) is the Lorentz transformation matrix corresponding to the representation of the field operator. Correspondingly, we have \(\mathcal{F}'(x)=S(\Lambda)\mathcal{F}(x+\delta x)\).

Physically meaningful transformations preserve the norm of states and therefore can be written in the form of unitary operators (or antiunitary operators in the case of time inversal), namely, operators satisfying \(U^\dagger=U^{-1}\). We will take the transformation of states to be

\[|a\rangle\rightarrow |a'\rangle=U^\dagger|a\rangle,~~|b\rangle\rightarrow |b'\rangle=U^\dagger|b\rangle\]

We now have \(\langle a'|\phi(x')|b'\rangle=\langle a|U\phi(x')U^\dagger|b\rangle=\langle a|\phi'(x')|b\rangle\), which implies \(\phi'(x)=U\phi(x)U^\dagger\).

Now we are ready to introduce the charges generating the transformations. Assume that the transformation we are interested in are parametrized by a set of \(n\) infinitesimal parameters \(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2,\cdots,\epsilon_n\), in which case there are necessarily \(n\) charges \(Q_1,Q_2,\cdots,Q_n\). The charges are defined through

\[U=\exp(\pm i\epsilon_iQ_i)=\exp(\pm i\epsilon\cdot Q)\]

We get

\[\begin{aligned} \phi'(x)&\approx(1+\pm i\epsilon\cdot Q)\phi(x)(1\mp i\epsilon\cdot Q)\\ &=\phi(x)\pm i\epsilon\cdot Q\phi(x)\mp i\phi(x)\epsilon\cdot Q\\ &=\phi(x)\pm i[\epsilon\cdot Q,\phi(x)] \end{aligned}\]

Let’s define \(\delta\phi(x)=\phi'(x)-\phi(x)\), we obtain a very useful result:

\[\pm[\epsilon\cdot Q,\phi]=-i\delta\phi\]

where the sign on the left-hand side is the same sign as the argument of the exponential in the operator \(U\).

In the case of spacetime symmetries, for a scalar field, we have \(\delta\phi=\phi(x+\delta x)-\phi(x)\), for a general field \(\mathcal{F}\), we have instead \(\delta\mathcal{F}(x)=S(\Lambda)\mathcal{F}(x+\delta x)-\mathcal{F}(x)\).

As a simple example, consider a lagrangian density that is a function of a single real scalar field \(\mathcal{L}(\phi)\). The action \(S=i\int \mathrm{d}^4x\mathcal{L}\) is obviously invariant under a transformation of the field

\[\phi(x^\mu)\rightarrow \phi'(x^\mu)=\phi(x^\mu+a^\mu)\]

where \(a^\mu\) is a constant-displacement four-vector. Therefore

\[\delta\phi(x^\mu)=\phi(x^\mu+a^\mu)-\phi(x^\mu)\approx a^\mu\partial_\mu\phi\]

Since there are four parameters, the four \(a_\mu\), we introduce four charges that we suggestively call \(P^\mu\), and write the unitary operator as

\[U=\exp(ia^\mu P_\mu)\]

so we get

\[a^\mu[P_\mu,\phi]=-ia^\mu\partial_\mu\phi\Rightarrow [P_\mu,\phi]=-i\partial_\mu\phi\]

We next consider Lorentz transformation \(x^\mu\rightarrow \Lambda^\mu_\nu x^\nu\), for an infinitesimal transformation, we may write

\[\begin{aligned} \Lambda^\mu_\nu x^\nu&\approx x^\mu+\omega^\mu_\nu x^\nu\\ &=x^\mu+\omega^{\mu\nu}x_\nu \end{aligned}\]

where \(\omega^{\mu\nu}\) is antisymmetric, \(\omega^{\mu\nu}=-\omega^{\nu\mu}\).

We write the unitary operator implementing Lorentz transformation on a scalar field as

\[U=\exp\left(-\frac{i}{2}\omega^{\mu\nu}M_{\mu\nu}\right)\]

where the charges \(M^{\mu\nu}\) are also antisymmetric.

The transformation og the field is given by \(\delta\phi=\phi(x+\omega^{\mu\nu}x_\nu)-\phi(x^\mu)\), and \(\delta\phi=\frac{1}{2}\omega^{\mu\nu}(x_\nu\partial_\mu-x_\mu\partial_\nu)\phi\), so we have

\[[M_{\mu\nu},\phi]=i(x_\nu\partial_\nu-x_\mu\partial_\nu)\phi\]

We finally consider the \(U(1)\) transformation \(\phi\rightarrow e^{i\alpha}\phi\) with \(\alpha\) being a constant parameter, in this case, \(\delta\phi=i\alpha\phi\). Writing \(U=\exp(i\alpha Q)\), we have

\[[Q,\phi]=\phi\]

Explict Representations of the Charges and the Charge Algebra

The first strategy thet one can work out the algebra of the charges are the following steps:

\[\rm{Field~ transformations}\Rightarrow \rm{currents}\Rightarrow\rm{charges~as~quantum~fields}\Rightarrow\rm{algebra}\]

It consists of building a set of currents \(J_\mu^i\) associated with the symmetry from which the corresponding charges are found by integrating over space thezeroth component of the currents:

\[Q^i=\int\mathrm{d}^3xJ_0^i(\vec{x},t)\]

It is important to note that in this approach, the charges (and the currents) are themselves quantum field operators. The algebra of the charges then can be found using the equal time commutation relations of the fields present in the theory.

In the case of spacetime symmetries, there is a second explict representation of the charges available: as differential operators (not quantum field operators) acting on the fields. The differential operator representation of the charge is defined via

\[\begin{aligned} \phi(x')&=\exp(\pm i\epsilon_i\hat{Q}_i)\phi(x)\\ &\approx\phi(x)\pm i\epsilon_i\hat{Q}_i\phi(x) \end{aligned}\]

We first consider \(U=\exp(-ia^\mu \hat{P}_\mu)\), we have \(\phi(x)+a^\mu\partial_\mu\phi(x)=\phi(x)-ia^\mu\hat{P}_\mu\phi(x)\), which leads to

\[\hat{P}_\mu=i\partial_\mu\]

For the differential operators \(\hat{M}_{\mu\nu}\) on a scalar field, we have

\[\phi(x')=\exp\left(\frac{i}{2}\omega^{\mu\nu}\hat{M}_{\mu\nu}\right)\phi\]

we can prove that

\[\hat{M}_{\mu\nu}=i(x_\mu\partial_\nu-x_\nu\partial_\mu)\]

Finally, we can use the representations of the operators \(P^\mu\) and \(M^{\mu\nu}\) as differential operators to show that they obey the so-called Poincare algebra:

\[\begin{aligned} [P_\mu,P_\nu]&=0\\ [M_{\mu\nu},P_\lambda]&=i(\eta_{\nu\lambda}P_\mu-\eta_{\lambda\mu}P_{\nu})\\ [M_{\mu\nu},M_{\rho\sigma}]&=i(\eta_{\nu\rho}M_{\mu\sigma}-\eta_{\nu\sigma}M_{\mu\rho}-\eta_{\mu\rho}M_{\nu\sigma}+\eta_{\mu\sigma}M_{\nu\rho}) \end{aligned}\]

Finding the Algebra Without the Explicit Charges

In this section, all the charges have to be thought of as quantum field operators, not differential operators.

We first consider two successive transformations applied to a field \(\phi\): the first transformation with parameters \(\alpha_i\), followed by the second tranformation with parameters \(\beta_j\). We have:

\[\begin{aligned} U_\beta U_\alpha\phi U_\alpha^\dagger U_\beta^\dagger&=\exp(i\beta\cdot Q)\exp(i\alpha\cdot Q)\phi\exp(-i\alpha\cdot Q)\exp(-i\beta\cdot Q)\\ &\approx \phi+i[\beta\cdot Q,\phi]+i[\alpha\cdot Q,\phi]-[\beta\cdot Q,[\alpha\cdot Q,\phi]]+\cdots \end{aligned}\]

We write

\[U_\beta U_\alpha\phi U_\alpha^\dagger U_\beta^\dagger=\delta_\beta\delta_\alpha\phi\]

Consider

\[\delta_\beta\delta_\alpha\phi-\delta_\alpha\delta_\beta\phi\]

which is equal to

\[U_\beta U_\alpha\phi U_\alpha^\dagger U_\beta^\dagger- U_\alpha U_\beta\phi U_\beta^\dagger U_\alpha^\dagger\approx -[\beta\cdot Q,[\alpha\cdot Q,\phi]]+[\alpha\cdot Q,[\beta\cdot Q,\phi]]\]

If we expand this out, being careful about the order of all the quantities involved, we find that half the terms cancel out, leaving

\[\delta_\beta\delta_\alpha\phi-\delta_\alpha\delta_\beta\phi=[[\alpha\cdot Q,\beta\cdot Q],\phi]\]

The SUSY Algebra

There is the two-component spinor \(\zeta\) and its complex conjugate \(\zeta^*\) for a total of four parameters. We therefore need four charges \(Q_1,Q_2,Q_1^\dagger,Q_2^\dagger\), which we can group into a Weyl spinor, that we will call \(Q\), and its hermitian conjugate \(Q^\dagger\). These SUSY charges are also referred to as supercharges.

We choose to make \(Q\) a left-chiral spinor, so we have

\[Q\cdots\zeta=Q(-i\sigma^2)\zeta,~~\overline{Q}\cdot\overline{\zeta}=Q^\dagger i\sigma^2\zeta^*\]

(recall that \(\zeta\) is not a quantum field operator, so we write \(\zeta^{\dagger T}\) simply as \(\zeta^*\)).

The unitary operator \(U\) generating SUSY transformations therefore is given by

\[U_\zeta=\exp(iQ\cdot\zeta+i\overline{Q}\cdot\overline{\zeta})\]

We get

\[\begin{aligned} [Q\cdot\zeta+\overline{Q}\cdot\overline{\zeta},\phi]&=-i\zeta\cdot\chi\\ [Q\cdot\zeta+\overline{Q}\cdot\overline{\zeta},\chi]&=-i(\partial_\mu\phi)\sigma^\mu\sigma^2\zeta^*\\ \end{aligned}\]

which implies

\[\begin{aligned} [Q\cdot\zeta,\phi]&=-i\zeta\cdot\chi\\ [\overline{Q}\cdot\overline{\zeta},\chi]&=-i(\partial_\mu\phi)\sigma^\mu\sigma^2\zeta^*\\ [\overline{Q}\cdot\overline{\zeta},\phi]&=[Q\cdot\zeta,\chi]=0 \end{aligned}\]

Applications of the SUSY Algebra

Classification of States Using the Algebra: Review of the Poincare Group

We first briefly review some basic representations og the Poincare group on one particle states.

Casimir operator are operators that commute with all the generators of the group. These operators are of key importance in building representations because their eigenvalues can be used to classify the representations of the group. One Casimir operator of the Poincare group is the squared momentum generator \(P^\mu P_\mu\), and this obviously commutes with the momentum generators. The eigenvalue of \(P^\mu P_\mu\) is \(m^2\), which is therefore one of the parameters used to classify one-particle states.

The second Casimir operator is build out of the so-called Pauli-Lubanski operator \(W_\mu\), defined as

\[W^\mu=\frac{1}{2}\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}M_{\rho\sigma}P_\nu\]

where \(\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\) is the totally antisymmetric Levi-Civita symbol in four dimensions. We will use the normalization \(\epsilon_{0123}=1\), which implies \(\epsilon^{0123}=-1\). With this normalization, it’s clear that \(\epsilon^{0ijk}=-\epsilon^{ijk}\).

Note that we have

\[W^\mu P_\mu=\frac{1}{2}\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}M_{\rho\sigma}P_\nu P_\mu=0\]

which is obvious beacuse \(P_\nu P_\mu\) is symmetric. But it is not as obvious that \(P_\mu W^\mu\) is also equal to zero, and we have \([P_\mu,W^\mu]=0\).

With some effort, one can show that \(W^\mu W_\mu\) also commutes with all the generators of the Poincare group, so it is our second Casimir operator.

To uncover the physicial signification of the Pauli-Lubanski operator, consider first massive particles. In that case, we can choose to work in the rest frame of the particle, where its four-momentum is simply \(p^\mu=p_\mu=(m,\vec{0})\), so we have

\[P_\mu|p\rangle=P_0|p\rangle=m|p\rangle\]

Let us now apply the Pauli-Lubanski operator to this state

\[\begin{aligned} W^\mu|p\rangle&=\frac{1}{2}\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}M_{\rho\sigma}P_\nu|p\rangle\\ &=\frac{1}{2}\epsilon^{\mu 0\rho\sigma}M_{\rho\sigma}P_0|p\rangle\\ &=\frac{m}{2}\epsilon^{\mu 0\rho\sigma}M_{\rho\sigma}|p\rangle\\ \end{aligned}\]

We can essily see that

\[W^0|p\rangle=0\]

and

\[\begin{aligned} W^i|p\rangle&=\frac{m}{2}\epsilon^{i0jk}M_{jk}|p\rangle\\ &=-\frac{m}{2}\epsilon^{0ijk}M_{jk}|p\rangle\\ &=\frac{m}{2}\epsilon^{ijk}M_{jk}|p\rangle\\ \end{aligned}\]

It turns out that the operator \(frac{1}{2}\epsilon^{ijk}M_{jk}\) is simply the total angular momentum of the particle, i.e.,

\[\frac{1}{2}\epsilon^{ijk}M_{jk}=L^i+S^i\]

We therefore have

\[W^i|p\rangle=m(S^i+L^i)|p\rangle\]

which, in the rest frame, reduced to

\[W^i|p\rangle=mS^i|p\rangle\]

So the Casimir operator \(W_\mu W^\mu\) acting on this state gives

\[W_\mu W^\mu|p\rangle=W^0 W^0|p\rangle-W^iW^i|p\rangle=-m^2\vec{S}^2|p\rangle=-m^2s(s+1)|p\rangle\]

We now see that the second porperty that can be used to specify the masssive representation of the Lorentz group is the spin (or total angular momentum, if we are not working in the rest frame).

To classify all these possible states of a massive particle at rest, we need a complete set of commuting observables. In addition to the four-momentum squared (whose eigenvalue is \(m^2\)) and the square of the spin, we also may use the component of the spin along the z axis, \(S_z\), because this operator obviously commutes with \(P_\mu P^\mu\) and with \(\vec{S}^2\). Note that

\[W^3=m(L_z+S_z)\]

which, in the rest frame of the particle, reduces to \(W^3=mS_z\).

Therefore, massive states are labeled in their rest frame by three quantum numbers: \(p,s,s_z\), with

\[\begin{aligned} P^\mu|p,s,s_z\rangle&=m|p,s,s_z\rangle\\ W_\mu W^\mu|p,s,s_z\rangle&=-m^2\vec{S}^2|p,s,s_z\rangle\\ &=-m^2s(s+1)|p,s,s_z\rangle\\ \frac{W^3}{m}|p,s,s_z\rangle&=S_z|p,s,s_z\rangle=s_z|p,s,s_z\rangle \end{aligned}\]

As we know from quantum mechanics, \(s_z\) may take \(2s+1\) values, ranging from \(-s\) to \(+s\) in integer steps.

Now consider massless particles. In this case, we cannot go to the rest frame of the particle, but we may choose to work in a frame where the four-momentum is given by

\[p^\mu=(E,0,0,E)\]

We will denote the corresponding stste by \(|p\rangle_0\). We then have

\[\begin{aligned} P^\mu|p\rangle_0&=(P^0,P^1,P^2,P^3)|p\rangle_0\\ &=(E,0,0,E)|p\rangle_0 \end{aligned}\]

or

\[\begin{aligned} &P^1|p\rangle_0=P^2|p\rangle_0=0\\ &P^0|p\rangle_0=P^3|p\rangle_0=E|p\rangle_0 \end{aligned}\]

We can write \(P^\mu|p\rangle_0=(P^3,0,0,P^3)|p\rangle_0\).

It turns out that like \(P^\mu P_\mu, W^\mu W_\mu\) gives zero when acting on a massless one-particle state. The proof can be found in Wigner’s original paper. The fact that \(W^\mu W_\mu\) gives zero when applied to a one-particle massless state, together with \(W^\mu P_\mu=0\), implies that when acting on such a state, the operator \(W^\mu\) is proportional to the four-momentum operator

\[W^\mu|p\rangle_0=hP^\mu|p\rangle_0\]

Now we prove this, consider

\[\begin{aligned} W^\mu P_\mu|p\rangle_0&=(W^0P^0-W^1P^1-W^2P^2-W^3P^3)|p\rangle_0\\ &=(W^0P^0-W^3P^3)|p\rangle_0\\ &=E(W^0-W^3)|p\rangle_0=0 \end{aligned}\]

so we conclude that

\[W^0|p\rangle_0=W^3|p\rangle_0\]

Using this we have

\[\begin{aligned} W^\mu W_\mu&=(W^0W_0-W^1W_1-W^2W_2-W^3W_3)|p\rangle_0\\ &=-(W^1W_1+W^2W_2)|p\rangle_0=0 \end{aligned}\]

so we obtain

\[W^1|p\rangle_0=W^2|p\rangle_0=0\]

Therefore, when acting on the state, the action of the operator \(W^\mu\) reduces to

\[W^\mu|p\rangle_0=(W^3,0,0,W^3)|p\rangle_0\]

From \([P_\mu,W^\mu]=0\) we know that \(W^3\) commutes with \(P^3\), so we may take \(|p\rangle_0\) to be a common eigenstate of these two operators. This implise that we may write

\[W^3|p\rangle_0=hP^3|p\rangle_0\]

Using \(P^\mu|p\rangle_0=(P^3,0,0,P^3)|p\rangle_0\) and \(W^\mu|p\rangle_0=(W^3,0,0,W^3)|p\rangle_0\) we can conclude that

\[W^\mu|p\rangle_0=hP^\mu|p\rangle_0\]

as we had set out to prove.

To find out what the constant \(h\) represents, we consider

\[\begin{aligned} W^\mu|p\rangle_0&=(hE,0,0,hE)|p\rangle_0\\ \Leftrightarrow ~~~~~~~~~~~~~W^3|p\rangle_0&=hE|p\rangle_0\\ \Leftrightarrow E(S_z+L_z)|p\rangle_0&=hE|p\rangle_0\\ \Leftrightarrow ~~~~~~~~~~~~Es_z|p\rangle_0&=hE|p\rangle_0\\ \Leftrightarrow ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~h&=s_z \end{aligned}\]

Since our state has its momentum along the z direction, we may write

\[h=\vec{s}\cdot\hat{p}\]

which is nothing other than the helicity of the perticle.

So massless representations of the Lorentz group are therefore completely specified by only two numbers: their energy (which then specifies their four-momentum) and their helicity \(h\). The reason we don’t have \(2s+1\) states lies in \(W^1|p\rangle_0=W^2|p\rangle_0=0\), which reveals that we do not have any ladder operators to change the z component of the spin. Therefore, there is only one spin state.
